# Lincoln Innovation Academy
# Equity Audit Report
## School Year 2023-2024

**Conducted by:** Equity Committee (Dr. Angela Rivera, Chair)
**Committee Members:** Sarah Mitchell, Marcus Davis, Grace Nkomo, Tamara Williams, Maria Santos, Dr. Marcus Webb (Board Member)
**External Facilitator:** Dr. Amina Hassan, Equity in Education Consulting
**Date Completed:** August 2024

---

## Purpose

This equity audit was conducted to assess Lincoln Innovation Academy's policies, practices, and outcomes through an equity lens, identifying areas of strength and areas where disparities exist across racial, socioeconomic, and other demographic groups. The audit examines the 2023-2024 school year data and informs the school's strategic planning and continuous improvement processes.

---

## Methodology

The audit examined five domains:
1. Academic Achievement and Access
2. Discipline and School Culture
3. Staffing and Representation
4. Family and Community Engagement
5. Curriculum and Instruction

Data sources included:
- Student achievement data (MAP, MCA, classroom grades) disaggregated by race/ethnicity, FRL status, SpEd status, and ELL status
- Discipline records disaggregated by demographics
- Staff demographic data and retention data
- Family survey responses disaggregated by demographics
- Curriculum audit of representation in texts and materials
- Classroom observation data
- Focus groups with students, families, and staff

## 1. Academic Achievement and Access

### Key Findings

**MCA Proficiency Rates by Race/Ethnicity (2023-2024, Grades 3-8)**

| Group | ELA Proficiency | Math Proficiency | Science Proficiency (5 & 8) |
|---|---|---|---|
| All Students | 52% | 45% | 41% |
| White Students (24%) | 68% | 62% | 58% |
| Black Students (38%) | 42% | 34% | 30% |
| Hispanic/Latino Students (22%) | 48% | 40% | 36% |
| Asian Students (8%) | 62% | 58% | 52% |
| Multiracial Students (8%) | 55% | 48% | 44% |
| Free/Reduced Lunch (66%) | 44% | 37% | 33% |
| Non-FRL (34%) | 68% | 60% | 56% |
| Students with IEPs (13%) | 22% | 18% | 15% |
| English Learners (22%) | 30% | 32% | 24% |

**Observations:**
- Significant achievement gaps persist between White and Black students (26 points in ELA, 28 points in math)
- FRL status is a strong predictor of achievement, with a 24-point gap in ELA and 23-point gap in math
- Students with IEPs have the largest gaps, with proficiency rates less than half the school average
- English Learners show lower proficiency in ELA (expected given language development) but also significant gaps in math and science
- LIA's overall proficiency rates exceed the state average for charter schools (ELA: 52% vs 46%, Math: 45% vs 39%)

**Growth Data (MAP, Percentage Meeting Growth Projection)**

| Group | ELA Growth % Meeting | Math Growth % Meeting |
|---|---|---|
| All Students | 62% | 58% |
| White Students | 65% | 63% |
| Black Students | 58% | 52% |
| Hispanic/Latino Students | 60% | 55% |
| Asian Students | 68% | 65% |
| FRL Students | 59% | 54% |
| Students with IEPs | 50% | 44% |
| English Learners | 64% | 56% |

**Observations:**
- Growth gaps are narrower than proficiency gaps, suggesting instruction is effective but not yet closing gaps at a sufficient rate
- English Learners show strong ELA growth (64%), indicating language acquisition supports are working
- Students with IEPs show the lowest growth rates, suggesting need for more intensive and effective interventions
- Black students in math growth (52%) is a particular area of concern

**Access to Advanced/Enrichment Opportunities**

| Opportunity | School Enrollment % Students of Color | Participation % Students of Color |
|---|---|---|
| Math Enrichment Group (4-8) | 76% | 58% |
| Reading Enrichment/Book Clubs | 76% | 62% |
| STEM After-School Club | 76% | 70% |
| Student Council | 76% | 72% |
| PBL Showcase Leadership Roles | 76% | 65% |

Students of color are underrepresented in academic enrichment opportunities, particularly math enrichment (58% vs 76% enrollment). STEM club and student council show better representation, likely due to intentional recruitment efforts.

### Recommendations
1. Implement universal screening for enrichment/advanced opportunities rather than teacher nomination only
2. Set explicit growth targets for Black students in math (target: 60% meeting growth projection by 2025-26)
3. Review SpEd service delivery model to improve achievement growth for students with IEPs
4. Investigate barriers to enrichment access for students of color (schedule conflicts, transportation, awareness)

## 2. Discipline and School Culture

### Key Findings

**Office Discipline Referrals by Race/Ethnicity (2023-2024)**

| Group | % of Student Body | % of Office Referrals | Referral Rate (per 100 students) | Suspension Rate |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| White | 24% | 16% | 8.2 | 0.0% |
| Black | 38% | 52% | 16.8 | 4.2% |
| Hispanic/Latino | 22% | 20% | 11.1 | 1.2% |
| Asian | 8% | 4% | 6.1 | 0.0% |
| Multiracial | 8% | 8% | 12.3 | 0.0% |

**Observations:**
- Black students receive office discipline referrals at more than twice the rate of White students (16.8 vs 8.2 per 100 students)
- Black students represent 38% of the student body but 52% of office referrals - a clear disproportionality
- Black students are the only group with out-of-school suspensions (4.2% suspension rate)
- Boys across all racial groups receive referrals at 2.4x the rate of girls
- The disproportionality is most pronounced in "defiance/insubordination" referrals and less pronounced in more objective categories

**Restorative Practice Usage:**
- 62% of discipline incidents included a restorative practice component (target: 75%)
- Teachers with restorative practices training showed 30% fewer referrals than untrained teachers
- Student focus group feedback indicated that restorative circles "help sometimes but not always" and several Black male students reported feeling that behavior expectations were applied inconsistently across classrooms

### Recommendations
1. Provide additional implicit bias training for all staff, with specific focus on subjective referral categories (defiance, disruption)
2. Achieve 100% staff completion of restorative practices foundational training by end of 2024-25
3. Implement referral review process: principal reviews all referrals weekly with attention to demographic patterns
4. Establish peer mediation program for middle school students
5. Collect and analyze student perception data on fairness of discipline practices, disaggregated by race

## 3. Staffing and Representation

### Key Findings

**Staff Demographics vs. Student Demographics**

| Group | Student % | Teaching Staff % | All Staff % |
|---|---|---|---|
| White | 24% | 65% | 58% |
| Black | 38% | 12% | 18% |
| Hispanic/Latino | 22% | 12% | 14% |
| Asian | 8% | 8% | 6% |
| Multiracial | 8% | 3% | 4% |

**Observations:**
- Significant gap between student diversity (76% students of color) and teacher diversity (35% teachers of color)
- The school has made progress: teachers of color increased from 24% in 2020-21 to 35% in 2023-24
- Non-teaching staff (paras, office, custodial, food service) are more racially diverse (48% people of color)
- All administrators/coaches are White except one instructional coach (Black) and the family liaison (Black)
- Teacher retention is slightly lower for teachers of color (82%) than White teachers (90%) over the past 3 years

**Staff Survey on Equity (selected items, N=35):**
- "LIA is committed to equity and inclusion": 88% agree
- "I feel comfortable discussing race and equity with colleagues": 72% agree
- "I have received adequate training to address equity in my classroom": 64% agree
- "Staff of color are valued and supported at LIA": 76% agree (but only 58% of staff of color agreed)
- "Students of all racial backgrounds receive equitable treatment": 78% agree

### Recommendations
1. Set hiring target: 50% of new hires should be people of color (current pipeline strategies are working but need acceleration)
2. Conduct stay interviews with staff of color to understand retention barriers
3. Create affinity space/support group for staff of color
4. Ensure interview panels always include at least one person of color
5. Develop leadership pipeline that intentionally develops leaders of color

## 4. Family and Community Engagement

### Key Findings

**Family Survey Response Rates by Demographic Group**

| Group | Response Rate | Overall Satisfaction (Avg 1-5) |
|---|---|---|
| White Families | 72% | 4.4 |
| Black Families | 48% | 4.0 |
| Hispanic/Latino Families | 42% | 4.1 |
| Asian Families | 55% | 4.3 |
| FRL Families | 44% | 3.9 |
| Non-FRL Families | 68% | 4.4 |
| ELL Families | 38% | 3.8 |

**Event Attendance (Average)**

| Event Type | Overall Attendance % | White Family % | Black Family % | Hispanic Family % |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Back to School Night | 78% | 88% | 72% | 70% |
| Parent-Teacher Conferences | 85% | 92% | 80% | 78% |
| PBL Showcase | 72% | 85% | 65% | 62% |
| Family Movie Night | 55% | 50% | 60% | 58% |
| Cultural Celebration Events | 65% | 55% | 72% | 70% |

**Observations:**
- Significant gap in survey response rates between White families (72%) and families of color (42-55%), suggesting the school's engagement methods may not be equally accessible
- ELL families have the lowest response rate (38%) and satisfaction (3.8) despite multilingual survey options being available
- Informal, social events (movie night, cultural celebrations) show more equitable or even reversed attendance patterns, suggesting that formal school events may feel less welcoming to some families
- PTA leadership: 8 of 10 PTA board members are White, despite White families comprising only 24% of the school community
- Families receiving interpretation services report higher satisfaction with the school when they have consistent, designated interpreter access

### Recommendations
1. Redesign family survey distribution: add phone-based survey option, offer incentives, send through multiple channels including text
2. Diversify PTA leadership through intentional recruitment and mentorship
3. Shift event formats: include more informal, community-building events alongside academic-focused events
4. Assign designated bilingual liaisons (not just interpreters) for Somali, Hmong, and Karen-speaking families
5. Conduct family focus groups in home languages to understand engagement barriers more deeply

## 5. Curriculum and Instruction

### Key Findings

**Curriculum Representation Audit (conducted by Equity Committee, sample of K-8 ELA texts)**

| Metric | Finding |
|---|---|
| Authors of color in ELA curriculum | 38% (K-5: 42%, 6-8: 34%) |
| Protagonists of color | 45% |
| Books featuring non-stereotypical portrayals of communities of color | 72% of books with characters of color |
| Representation of disability | 8% of texts |
| LGBTQ+ representation | 3% of texts (all in grades 6-8) |
| Representation of immigrant/refugee experiences | 15% of texts |
| Non-Western perspectives in social studies | Limited - primarily Western-centric through grade 5 |

**Classroom Observation Data (Equity Focus, N=36 observations across 18 teachers)**

| Practice Observed | % of Observations Where Present |
|---|---|
| Culturally relevant examples and connections | 42% |
| Equitable participation strategies (cold calling, random selection) | 56% |
| Multiple means of representation (UDL) | 64% |
| Student identity affirmed in classroom environment | 72% |
| Higher-order questions directed equitably across student groups | 38% |
| Collaborative structures with intentional grouping | 58% |

**Observations:**
- Curriculum representation has improved with recent adoptions but still does not fully mirror the student population
- Culturally relevant teaching practices are present but inconsistent (42% of observations)
- Higher-order questioning is the weakest equity indicator - teachers tend to ask more rigorous questions of students they perceive as higher-performing, which correlates with race
- Social studies curriculum in K-5 is significantly more Western-centric than the student population warrants
- New CRT professional development series (Dr. Amina Hassan, 2024-25) is directly addressing many of these gaps

### Recommendations
1. Complete curriculum representation audit across all subject areas (currently only ELA is audited)
2. Set target: 50%+ authors/protagonists of color in ELA by 2025-26
3. Supplement social studies curriculum with non-Western perspectives, particularly African, Latin American, and Southeast Asian content relevant to student backgrounds
4. Continue CRT professional development and add classroom coaching component
5. Train all teachers on equitable questioning strategies with observation follow-up

## Summary of Priority Actions

### Immediate (2024-25)
1. Implement implicit bias training for all staff (December 2024)
2. Begin weekly discipline referral review with demographic analysis
3. Redesign family survey for more equitable response rates
4. Complete CRT professional development series
5. Conduct stay interviews with staff of color

### Short-Term (2025-26)
1. Universal screening for enrichment opportunities
2. Hire additional teachers of color (50% target for new hires)
3. Complete curriculum representation audit across all subjects
4. Establish peer mediation program
5. Create staff of color affinity group

### Long-Term (2026-27)
1. Close achievement growth gap to within 5 percentage points across racial groups
2. Eliminate discipline disproportionality (referral rate ratio below 1.5)
3. Achieve 50%+ teachers of color
4. Achieve equitable family engagement across demographic groups
5. Fully representative curriculum across all subject areas

---

## Accountability

The Equity Committee will meet monthly to monitor progress on these recommendations. Data dashboards will be reviewed quarterly by the school leadership team and shared with the Board of Directors. An updated equity audit will be conducted in 2026-27.
