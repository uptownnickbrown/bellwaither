LINCOLN INNOVATION ACADEMY
STUDENT FOCUS GROUP TRANSCRIPT
Middle School Students (Grades 6-8)

Date: January 22, 2025
Time: 12:15 PM - 12:55 PM (during lunch period, with student consent)
Location: Media Center Conference Room

Facilitator: Mr. Pham (School Counselor, Grades 5-8)
Note Taker: Ms. Tamara Williams (Family Liaison)

Participants (names changed to protect student identity):
- Student A: 6th grade, Female, has attended LIA since kindergarten
- Student B: 7th grade, Male, has attended LIA since 3rd grade
- Student C: 8th grade, Female, has attended LIA since 6th grade
- Student D: 6th grade, Male, has attended LIA since kindergarten
- Student E: 7th grade, Female, has attended LIA since 5th grade
- Student F: 8th grade, Male, has attended LIA since kindergarten
- Student G: 7th grade, Non-binary, has attended LIA since 7th grade (1st year)
- Student H: 8th grade, Female, has attended LIA since kindergarten

Purpose: To gather student voice on school culture, academic experience, and areas for improvement as part of the school's continuous improvement process.

Note: Parental consent and student assent forms were collected prior to the focus group. Students were reminded that participation is voluntary and they can skip any question.

====================================================================

MR. PHAM: Thank you all for agreeing to spend part of your lunch with us today. I want to remind everyone that there are no wrong answers here, and everything you say will be kept confidential in terms of your real names. We want to hear your honest thoughts about your experience at Lincoln Innovation Academy. Let's start with something positive. What do you like most about going to school here?

STUDENT A: The teachers actually care about you. Like, at my cousin's school, the teachers just teach and leave. Here, Ms. Nkomo knows what's going on in my life. She asked about my grandmother when she was sick. That matters.

STUDENT F: I've been here since K, so this is basically my second home. I like that it's small. Everyone knows everyone. I can't get away with stuff but also people notice when I'm having a bad day.

STUDENT H: Same. I like that we do projects and not just worksheets all day. The PBL stuff in 8th grade is actually interesting. We're working on a documentary about gentrification in our neighborhood and it feels like it matters.

STUDENT B: I like the sports options even though we're small. The basketball team is fun. And the after-school STEM club with the robotics.

STUDENT G: I'm new this year and honestly it took a while to fit in because everyone already knew each other. But the advisory class helped. And the counselors check in a lot which is nice.

STUDENT C: I like that we get to have discussions about real stuff. In my ELA class we read "The Giver" and had a debate about freedom versus safety that was really deep.

STUDENT D: Free breakfast. (laughing) No but actually I like the art program. Mr. -- the art teacher -- lets us do real art, not just coloring sheets.

STUDENT E: The community circles. I know some kids think they're corny but when there's actually a problem, they help.

MR. PHAM: Great. Now let me ask you about the academic side. Do you feel challenged in your classes? Are you learning what you need to learn?

STUDENT H: Honestly, some classes are more challenging than others. Ms. Adeyemi's ELA class pushes me hard and I've become a way better writer. But some other classes feel too easy sometimes.

STUDENT F: Math is really hard for me and I don't feel like there's enough help. Like, the intervention block is helpful but it's only 30 minutes and by the time we get settled it's more like 20 minutes.

STUDENT B: I think the work is fair. Some weeks are really busy with projects and tests all at the same time though. Teachers don't seem to coordinate with each other on due dates.

STUDENT A: I feel like I'm learning a lot in 6th grade. The switch to having different teachers for different subjects was hard at first but now I like it. I feel more grown up.

STUDENT C: I wish we had more elective options. Like, I want to take a real coding class, not just Code.org during computer time. And we don't have a foreign language program.

STUDENT G: At my old school we had Spanish class starting in 6th grade. I miss that. It would be cool to have that here.

STUDENT D: I think the work could be harder in some classes. Sometimes I finish early and just have to read quietly or do extra worksheets. That's boring.

STUDENT E: The reading assignments are good but I wish we could pick our own books more. The whole-class novels are fine but I have different interests.

MR. PHAM: Several of you mentioned wanting more challenging work or more options. If you could add or change one thing about your academic experience, what would it be?

STUDENT C: Foreign language, definitely. High schools expect you to have some background and we have none.

STUDENT H: More Advanced options. Like advanced math or advanced ELA for kids who are ready.

STUDENT F: More time for intervention and more math support. Maybe after school tutoring.

STUDENT B: Better coordination between teachers on when things are due. A shared calendar or something.

STUDENT D: More hands-on science. We do projects but the actual lab stuff could be cooler.

STUDENT A: I agree with Student C about foreign language. And maybe more field trips connected to what we're learning.

STUDENT G: I'd like more group projects where we get to pick the groups. The assigned groups can be awkward.

STUDENT E: A school newspaper or journalism class. I like writing but not the kind of writing we always do in class.

MR. PHAM: Now let's talk about school culture. Do you feel like Lincoln Innovation Academy is a welcoming place for all students? Do you feel safe and respected?

STUDENT A: Mostly yes. There are friend groups and sometimes people are exclusive but the teachers don't tolerate bullying.

STUDENT F: I feel safe here. Like physically safe. The lockdown drills are kind of scary but I know the school takes security seriously.

STUDENT G: Honestly? It took me a long time to feel welcome. People were nice but kind of already had their friend groups. And I didn't feel like there was a space for kids who are... different. Like, when I asked a teacher to use they/them pronouns, she was nice about it but I could tell she didn't really get it. She still messes up sometimes.

MR. PHAM: Thank you for sharing that, Student G. That's really important feedback.

STUDENT B: I think it depends on who you are. I've heard kids say stuff that's kind of racist, like "joking" about someone's hair or skin color. Teachers don't always hear it or sometimes they say "be nice" but don't really address it.

STUDENT H: I've experienced that too. Like microaggressions. Someone asked to touch my hair in 6th grade and when I told the teacher she said they were "just curious." That doesn't feel great.

STUDENT E: The restorative circles are good when they happen but sometimes they feel forced. Like, you can tell the person doesn't actually mean their apology but they say it because they have to.

STUDENT D: I think the school is welcoming overall. The cultural celebration events are cool. I like seeing everyone's food and traditions.

STUDENT C: I feel safe but I think social media makes things complicated. Drama that starts online comes into school and it's hard for teachers to deal with because they don't really understand what's happening on social media.

STUDENT A: Yeah, the phone policy is so strict but I understand why. It's just hard because social media stuff doesn't stop just because you put your phone in the holder.

MR. PHAM: What would help make the school more welcoming and inclusive?

STUDENT G: Training for teachers on pronouns and LGBTQ+ stuff. Not just one training but like, ongoing. And maybe a GSA club or safe space club. I asked about starting one and the counselor said she'd look into it but nothing happened yet.

STUDENT B: I think teachers need to actually address the racist jokes and comments, not just say "be nice." Have a real conversation about why it's wrong.

STUDENT H: More representation in what we read and learn about. We read "To Kill a Mockingbird" which is about racism but it's from a White person's perspective. I want to read books by Black authors about Black experiences that aren't just about suffering.

STUDENT E: A student leadership thing where students can actually give input on school decisions. Not just student council that plans dances, but like a real voice.

STUDENT F: Maybe a mentorship program? Like older students paired with younger ones. I could help 6th graders the way some 8th graders helped me when I was struggling.

STUDENT D: More recess or free time. We need a break in the middle of the day that isn't just lunch.

STUDENT A: I agree about the books thing. Also in social studies we spend a lot of time on European history but a lot of us have roots in Africa, Latin America, or Asia. That should be reflected more.

MR. PHAM: Let's talk about your relationship with adults in the building. Do you feel like there's at least one adult here who you could go to if you had a problem?

STUDENT A: Yes. Ms. Nkomo. Also Ms. Tamara (waves at Williams).

STUDENT B: My basketball coach, Mr. Park. He's chill and easy to talk to.

STUDENT C: The counselors are great. Mr. Pham, you know you're one of them. (laughing)

STUDENT D: My homeroom teacher. Also the front office lady, Ms. Ng, always checks on me.

STUDENT E: Mr. Pham and also Ms. Taylor in ELA. She's a good listener.

STUDENT F: Ms. Adeyemi. She's strict but she's the realest teacher here. She doesn't treat me like a kid.

STUDENT G: Mr. Pham has been really helpful since I started. And the social work intern, Aisha, is great. I just wish she was here every day, not just three days a week.

STUDENT H: I have several adults I trust. But I know some kids don't have even one. There are some quiet kids who kind of slip through the cracks.

MR. PHAM: Last question. If you could say one thing directly to Dr. Rivera about making this school better, what would it be?

STUDENT A: Keep the small school feeling even as we grow. Don't add too many students.

STUDENT B: More sports and activities. And fix the gym floor, it's slippery.

STUDENT C: Start a foreign language program. And let us have more input into what we learn.

STUDENT D: More recess and outdoor time. And better lunch options.

STUDENT E: Listen to student voices more. Not just in focus groups but regularly. A monthly student advisory meeting or something.

STUDENT F: Don't forget about the kids who struggle. Sometimes it feels like the smart kids get all the attention in positive ways and the struggling kids only get attention when they're in trouble.

STUDENT G: Make the school more explicitly inclusive of LGBTQ+ students. Have a pride flag or something visible. Start a GSA. Train the teachers. It makes a difference.

STUDENT H: Keep doing what you're doing mostly. But push us harder academically. I want to be ready for high school. And keep listening to Black students about our experiences here. The equity work matters.

MR. PHAM: Thank you all so much. This was incredibly valuable and I promise your voices will be heard. Dr. Rivera and the leadership team will review these notes and think about how we can act on your feedback. Enjoy the rest of your lunch.

[END OF FOCUS GROUP]

====================================================================
FACILITATOR NOTES (Mr. Pham)
====================================================================

Key themes that emerged:

1. STRENGTHS: Strong teacher-student relationships; small school community; project-based learning; restorative practices (when done well); feeling physically safe

2. ACADEMIC: Desire for more rigor and options (foreign language, advanced courses, journalism/electives); uneven challenge across classes; need for better coordination of assignments/due dates; desire for more student choice in reading

3. CULTURE/INCLUSION: LGBTQ+ inclusivity is a gap - need GSA and staff training; racial microaggressions occur and aren't always addressed adequately; social media creates school culture challenges; desire for more diverse curriculum representation

4. STUDENT VOICE: Students want more meaningful input in school decisions; student council is seen as social planning, not real advocacy

5. SUPPORT: Intervention time feels insufficient; quiet/struggling students may be overlooked; mentorship between grade levels was desired

I recommend sharing these findings with the full leadership team and incorporating student voice themes into the school improvement plan. The GSA request should be prioritized - Student G has asked multiple times. I will follow up with Dr. Rivera this week.

- Tuan Pham, School Counselor
